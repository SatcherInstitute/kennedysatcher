Criminal Justice and Equity Virtual Roundtable – Session 1 Summary

January 24, 2022 / Blog, News

Criminal Justice and Equity: Bridging the Gaps

Session 1 of a five-part series of virtual roundtables dedicated to addressing the landscape of inequities that affect justice-involved individuals

 

Session 1: Policy Implementation & Data Use – January 18, 2022, from 11 am – 12:30 p.m. EST via Zoom

 

Atlanta, GA – Nearly 200 listeners signed into the Kennedy Satcher Center for Mental Health Equity’s (KSCMHE) inaugural, virtual roundtable entitled “Criminal Justice and Equity: Bridging the Gaps.” Listeners from across disciplines and from all over the world tuned in, including participants from academia, public health, students, legislators, activists, researchers, and allied health professionals. The virtual event by KSCMHE was sponsored by WellPath Care and invited panelists that represented a diverse, inclusive, and unique set of voices in the field, to speak on a myriad of issues that influence outcomes for individuals affected by the criminal justice system. The esteemed panelists, while leaders and experts in their field, also share a close and personal connection to equity work. The speakers engaged in an hour-long discussion and addressed how policies and the use of data may help or harm the movement to rectify inequities that affect justice-involved individuals.

 

The event began with a viewing of some fast facts and statistics that demonstrated some inequities within the criminal justice system. Audience members then heard welcome remarks from the KSCMHE director, Madhuri Jha, LCSW, MPH who highlighted her experience as a long-standing community-based clinician and the systemic injustices experienced by children, adults, and families that has increased the rates of incarceration within certain communities. Former Congressman and KSCMHE co-founder, Patrick J. Kennedy, spoke about the urgent need to envision a justice system that is focused on protecting marginalized communities and not revictimizing them. He also emphasized the value of having a nexus of comprehensive mental health services for the most marginalized especially due to the United States being one of the few industrialized nations with alarmingly high rates of incarceration. Lastly, Daniel Dawes, JD, Executive Director of the Satcher Health Leadership Institute, shared some reflections about Martin Luther King day including the progress made by the nation since the passing of Dr. King, the areas of improvement within the movement for health equity and justice, and the rare opportunity we presently have to dismantle once and for all the systems of inequity that have disproportionately impacted the most vulnerable populations.

 

After an energizing introduction, audience members listened into the panel of the day on Policy Implementation and Data Use. The panel featured the following experts:

  • LaShawn Warren, JD, Chief Policy Officer for Southern Poverty Law Center
  • Lourdes M. Rosado, President and General Counsel of LatinoJustice PRLDEF
  • John Hart, PHD Associate Director at Vera Institute for Justice
  • Megan Douglas, JD, Associate Director for Policy, Kennedy-Satcher Center for Mental Health Equity

 

Moderated by KSCMHE Director, Madhuri Jha, LCSW, MPH, the panelists discussed various points of urgency in the policy landscape, and how data influences funding, decision-making, legislation, etc. LaShawn Warren, the Chief Policy Officer for the Southern Poverty Law Center kicked off the discussion by pointing out the different ways in which policy making can be improved for justice involved individuals. She particularly emphasized the need for creating a new paradigm for public safety – one that is humane, restorative in nature, and centers the multi-dimensionality of individuals. Lourdes Rosado discussed the various ways in which data has helped community agencies move away from punitive programs and towards practices that were backed up by evidence to be more effective. She further highlighted the shift in narrative around justice involved individuals within the Latinx community as there has been a deeper understanding of the nation’s inherently oppressive systems. John Hart from Vera Institute of Justice added to the conversation on restorative justice and how it can be used as a guide to process and resolve conflict within communities. He spotlighted the idea of cultural healing and the idea of recognizing pain and harm caused by the criminal legal system. As a researcher as well, Dr. Hart made powerful points about the urgent need for equitable data collection on a systemic level and having the most impacted to be on the frontlines of the research. Megan Douglas, Associate Director for Policy at the Kennedy-Satcher Center expanded on her work in developing legal data sets that quantify the effects of laws and public policy – particularly their effects on health outcomes for communities.

 

Best practices such as diversion programs, the CAHOOTS program, psychoeducation, and face to face engagement and outreach within communities were also highlighted by the speakers. These programs were noted as proactive ways to divert individuals from incarceration in the first place. LaShawn Warren stressed the seismic shift needed within society to address root causes and the trauma that permeates the US systems, stating, “We can’t fix the windows when the house is burning down!” This was echoed by John Hart, who added that it is time for people in power to be reflective and engage in power sharing to ensure the most marginalized receive the care they deserve. Lourdes added the urgency of having people with lived experience present at the table when discussing equity and restoration as well. The panelists closed the discussion by sharing their tips for individuals looking to add to the health equity movement through actionable change:

  • vote
  • join local advisory boards
  • run for office
  • disseminate and demand information
  • inform yourselves on the data which shapes policy
