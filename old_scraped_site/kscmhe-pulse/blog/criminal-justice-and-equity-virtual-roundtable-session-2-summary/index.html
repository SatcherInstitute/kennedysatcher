Criminal Justice and Equity Virtual Roundtable – Session 2 Summary

February 21, 2022 / Blog, News

Criminal Justice and Equity: Bridging the Gaps Webinar Series Overview

“Criminal Justice and Equity: Bridging the Gaps”

A five-part series of virtual roundtables dedicated to addressing the landscape of inequities that affect justice involved individuals.

 

Hundreds of listeners are signing into the Kennedy Satcher Center for Mental Health Equity’s (KSCMHE) virtual roundtables entitled “Criminal Justice and Equity: Bridging the Gaps.” Global listeners from across disciplines are welcome to tune in, including participants in academia, public health, students, legislators, activists, researchers, and allied health professionals.

 

This series of virtual events by KSCMHE is sponsored by Wellpath Cares Foundation and invites panelists that represent a diverse, inclusive, and unique set of voices in the field, to speak on a myriad of issues that influence outcomes for individuals affected by the criminal justice system. The esteemed panelists, while leaders and experts in their field, also share a close and personal connection to equity work. The speakers engage in hour-long conversations and discuss how behavioral health inequities affect justice involved individuals.

 

Session 2: Behavioral Health Equity & Incarceration

February 15, 2022, from 11 am – 12:30 p.m. EST via Zoom

Session 2 began with welcome remarks from the KSCMHE Director, Madhuri Jha, LCSW, MPH who discussed her experience working with people who live with serious mental illness and face incarceration due to lack of care. Former Congressman and KSCMHE co-founder, Patrick J. Kennedy, discussed the potential of our criminal justice system to be reshaped into a public health system. He emphasized the urgent need for reliable data collection within the criminal justice system – data that is broken down by race, oppression-based trauma, and similar sensitives, so that we can create policies that can positively impact those at the receiving end of systemic disadvantages. Lastly, Dr. Arthur Evans, CEO of the American Psychological Association, reflected on his experience as a community psychologist and how many people he saw that were incarcerated and never sufficiently connected to the behavioral healthcare system. He shared poignant insights about challenging our false narratives about those that are adversely impacted by the justice system.

 

After an energizing introduction, audience members listened into the panel of the day on Behavioral Health Equity and Incarceration. The panel featured the following experts:

  • Jessica Klaver (New York City)– Senior Director of Behavioral Health Services at CASES (Center for Alternative Sentencing and Employment Services)
  • Nicole Palumbo, LCSW (New York City) – Special Consultant (Behavioral Health), NYC Department of Health and Mental Hygiene
  • Pierluigi Mancini (National) – President of the Multicultural Development Institute, Psychologist and Leading Consultant in Culturally Responsive Care
  • Chyna Quarker, MA (Atlanta) – Referral Manager for the Atlanta Policing Alternatives and Diversion Initiative
  • Cassandra Kirk, JD (Atlanta) – Chief Magistrate Judge, Magistrate Court of Fulton County

 

Moderated by KSCMHE Director, Madhuri Jha, LCSW, MPH, the panelists discussed various points of urgency in the behavioral health and criminal justice landscape, including the unique needs of justice involved individuals who live with mental health conditions. The panel aimed to highlight experiences with diversion programs and alternatives to incarceration as an effort to combat the prevalence of behavioral health misdiagnosis and criminalization of mental illness. Dr. Jessica Klaver, Senior Director of Behavioral Health Services at CASES in New York City kicked off the conversation at the roundtable by shedding some light on the multiple needs and priorities of incarcerated individuals upon release. She discussed the value of having more agencies such as CASES that aim to be a “one stop shop” in terms of service provision for the justice affected. CASES prioritizes the provision of integrated primary and behavioral health care to formerly incarcerated persons, and partners with city courts to ensure that these services are included in supervised release initiatives. Dr. Mancini, President of the Multicultural Development Institute discussed the need for culturally responsive language access to justice-affected individuals that are non-English speaking. He highlighted that many immigrants and refugees wind up in the criminal justice system due to language misunderstandings. He recalled a troubling example of Spanish-speaking woman who was placed in a 72 hour hold because “hearing voices” to her meant she heard the voice of the EMT worker assessing her, and was understood by the EMT worker as having auditory hallucinations. The panelists all agreed with him that the experience of being restrained and taken to an emergency room is very traumatizing. He added that many languages do not define trauma, behavioral health, and healthcare in the same way as they are regarded in English and provision of multilingual response can be crucial to alleviating negative outcomes. Dr. Mancini also urged the need to unpack the ingrained racism and discrimination within the criminal justice system. Judge Cassandra Kirk, Chief Magistrate Judge at the Fulton County Court recounted her experiences shifting from a prosecutor to defense attorney, noting that her primary job as a prosecutor was case closure and that justice was tempered by the number of people sent down the path of incarceration. As she transitioned to defense attorney and child advocate attorney, her perspective changed, especially as she worked with individuals she felt were dehumanized by the system. The panelists, then, discussed the matter of visibility and invisibility of justice-involved individuals, particularly how the criminal justice system moves from centering a defendant when they need to be visible (during trials and court proceedings) to their lack of access and visibility in prisons and jails.

 

Chyna Quarker, Referral Manager for the Atlanta Policing Alternatives & Diversion Initiative (PAD) discussed how allowing a justice-involved person to consent for behavioral health services under the guidance and observation of mental health clinicians can be a way of restoring agency/autonomy. Data collected by PAD shows that those who are diverted at the level of arrest and connected to mental health and community resources experience dramatically lower rates of recidivism. Chyna emphasized the historic lack of trust between BIPOC communities and the criminal legal system, which has caused a reluctance to engage with agencies such as PAD. She urged a more holistic approach to addressing behavioral health inequities. Nicole Palumbo, LCSW and Special Consultant for the NYC Department of Health & Mental Hygiene shared her experience with assisted outpatient treatment (AOT) and how it can work for some justice-involved individuals. AOT is the legal practice of implementing community-based mental health treatment under a civil court commitment. 47 states implement psychiatric mandates, but they vary greatly in their administration and success. Ms. Palumbo highlighted that in New York, AOT is found to be heavily reliant on the competencies of the mental health professionals engaging with the clients, which cause undue pressure to ensure that patients remain out of the hospital and away from situations of retraumatization due to medication mandates. Nicole and Chyna also discussed the urgent need for reliable data and data-driven evidence that does not lose the voices of the community members affected. The panelists closed the conversation by acknowledging how COVID-19 has given us a rare opportunity to gut-check our systems and the ongoing need for us to:

  • Challenge false narratives about what it means to have a criminal legal history & present information to families and communities using language that is accessible
  • Aim to create a “one stop shop” for the biopsychosocial needs of those who were previously incarcerated, so holistic and wrap-around care is available upon release from prison
  • Create workforce pathways to the formerly incarcerated to become peer specialists and executives at agencies that serve this population
  • Ensure formerly incarcerated people are part of the conversation, by prioritizing the inclusion of their voice in research, policy implementation, and program design.
